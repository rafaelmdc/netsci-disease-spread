\section{Results}\label{sec:results}
We read every outcome in one currency, the peak of active infection, and walk the staged protocol
of Section~\ref{sec:params} one factor at a time: how each disease \emph{spreads} with no
intervention, which node-targeting rule best \emph{defends}, how that winner scales with
\emph{dose}, and what closing routes (\emph{interdiction}) achieves. All runs are on Europe, with
the disease type and the realism ladder (air $\rightarrow$ $+$water $\rightarrow$ $+$land) as the
two cross-cutting axes. Every configuration is run as an ensemble of ten random seedings of the
initial infection; we report the mean and a $95\%$ confidence interval across the ensemble, so
absolute counts are comparative rather than predictive and every difference we read below is
resolved against seed variation.

\subsection{The transport networks}\label{sec:res-structure}
Structure sets what any epidemic or countermeasure can do, so we characterise the substrates before
intervening (Table~\ref{tab:structure}; the full descriptor set is Table~\ref{tab:structure-full}).
All are sparse (density $\le 0.033$) yet strongly small-world: the mean shortest path is only about
three hops ($\langle\ell\rangle = 2.7$ on Europe air, $3.2$ on the multilayer) while local clustering
stays high ($C \approx 0.5$), so any two cities lie a few steps apart and an outbreak can cross the
continent in weeks. The degree distribution is heavy-tailed across two orders of magnitude
(Figure~\ref{fig:degdist}, Appendix~\ref{app:structure}), with a heterogeneity ratio $\langle k^2\rangle/\langle k\rangle$ of
$91$--$121$; in a metapopulation this heterogeneity is what lowers the invasion threshold and gives
the busiest airports their outsized seeding role~\cite{colizza:invasion}.

Climbing the realism ladder reorganises that structure without changing its small-world character.
Adding land and ferry layers roughly triples the modelled population and flips the degree
assortativity from disassortative on air alone ($r=-0.19$) to mildly assortative on the multilayer
($r=+0.14$), as commuting joins cities of similar size; it stretches the diameter from $6$ to $11$
and lifts modularity from $Q=0.28$ to $0.35$ as national road and ferry clusters form.

Most consequential is how a city's degree relates to its betweenness. In most
cities the two rank together, so the busiest airports are also the main bridges and it scarcely
matters which a vaccination rule targets. The exceptions are \emph{anomalous gateways}: cities that
lie on a great many shortest paths (high betweenness) despite having only a handful of direct routes
(low degree), so they carry an epidemic across the continent far out of proportion to their traffic.
This is the anomalous centrality Guimer\`a et al.\ found in the worldwide air network, with
Anchorage, few flights but the gateway to remote Alaska, the canonical
example~\cite{guimera:anomalous}. How many a network has is a property of the region: the correlation
$\rho(\text{degree},\text{betweenness})$ spans a spectrum from a US-like regime where hubs and bridges
coincide and anomalies all but vanish~\cite{sun:anomalous} to a worldwide-like regime rich in gateways. Europe's air network is strongly correlated
($\rho=0.89$, US-like), but adding the land and ferry layers pulls it toward the anomalous end
($\rho=0.74$) and surfaces seventeen gateways on the flagship, low-degree bridge cities on the
Nordic and western periphery (Figure~\ref{fig:geo}; the degree--betweenness scatter is
Figure~\ref{fig:scatter} in Appendix~\ref{app:structure}). These gateways are the
\emph{only} cities where degree- and betweenness-targeting disagree, which makes them the whole
stake of the defence comparison below: if targeting bridges beats targeting hubs, it can only be
because it protects them.

\subsection{How each disease spreads}\label{sec:res-spread}
With no intervention every one of the five types takes off on every rung of the ladder
(Figure~\ref{fig:spread-curves}a). We report the peak as a fraction of the population, so that substrates
carrying different numbers of people are compared fairly. The peak then orders cleanly by dynamical
class: the immunizing measles type (SIR) is highest, at $59\%$ of the population ($95\%$ CI
$\pm1.4$) on air alone, followed by the endemic gonorrhea type (SIS, $28\%$) and the latent COVID
type (SEIR, $12\%$), with the waning-immunity influenza type (SEIRS, $4.0\%$) and the
isolation-controlled Ebola type (SEIQRD, $3.8\%$) lowest. The intervals are tight (at most $\pm2$
points on the measles type and under half a point on the milder ones), so the ordering is resolved
across seeds. Latency delays a peak and isolation caps it, so the two types that add those
mechanisms surge least, even though every type clears its invasion
threshold~\cite{colizza:invasion}.

Climbing the realism ladder moves that fraction only a little, and chiefly for the measles type (air
$59\%$, $+$water $57\%$, $+$land $63\%$); the milder types stay within half a point, which is why we
normalise. In absolute counts the peak roughly doubles from air to the full multilayer (measles
$117$M $\rightarrow$ $216$M; gonorrhea $56$M $\rightarrow$ $97$M), but that rise is almost entirely a
population effect: the land layer pulls inland, non-airport cities into the network, growing the
modelled population from $199$M to $342$M. Per capita, the ladder adds people who can be infected,
not severity. Ferries even lower the peak slightly (measles $59\% \rightarrow 57\%$),
because the coastal cities they add crest out of phase and desynchronise the surge, whereas land's
role is dynamical, synchronising a larger connected population through recurrent
commuting~\cite{balcan:recurrent} and bringing the peak forward (Figure~\ref{fig:spread-curves}b). The \emph{temporal} signature, by contrast, belongs to the
disease and not the substrate: the acute measles type peaks within about three weeks (day
$17$--$21$) and burns out, while the endemic gonorrhea type is still climbing toward its plateau at
the four-year horizon.

\subsection{Which vaccination wins}\label{sec:res-defend}
Holding the budget at $15$ immunised cities ($75\%$ coverage) on the multilayer flagship, targeting
the structural \emph{bridges} (betweenness) yields the largest peak reduction for every one of the
five diseases (Figure~\ref{fig:defense}): measles $12.5\%$, the latent, waning, and
isolation-controlled types $12.6$--$12.9\%$, and the endemic gonorrhea type $10.8\%$. Targeting the
busiest \emph{hubs} (degree) and the dense-cluster \emph{subgraph} rule (Section~\ref{sec:defend})
tie well below it ($7.0$--$9.3\%$), while random immunisation is barely distinguishable from doing
nothing ($\approx$$1\%$). The confidence intervals are a few tenths of a point, so the ordering
bridges~$>$~hubs~$\approx$~subgraph~$\gg$~random is unambiguous; it holds for all five disease types
and, in the underlying table, at all three rungs, so the win is not an artefact of one disease or
one substrate~\cite{kitsak:spreaders}. And the gateways are what decide it. Because degree and
betweenness diverge \emph{only} at those cities (Section~\ref{sec:res-structure}), betweenness's
roughly four-point edge over degree is precisely the containment the seventeen low-degree bridges
carry, which degree-targeting forgoes by ranking them too low to protect.

The two optimal-percolation rules put that mechanism to a further test: does a method beat degree
only if it can see past raw connectivity to the gateways? They split cleanly. Collective
Influence~\cite{morone:influence}, which scores a city by its neighbourhood's reach rather than its
own traffic, nearly matches betweenness (measles $11.8\%$ against $12.4\%$, and within half a point
on every disease), recovering most of the advantage that degree forfeits at the gateways. The
non-backtracking rule~\cite{radicchi:nonbacktracking}, by contrast, tracks degree ($7.8$--$9.2\%$):
its eigenvector ranking rewards the same busy cities degree already finds and so misses the same
gateways. The two rules that beat degree are exactly the two that can find the low-degree bridges,
and the one that cannot forfeits them alongside degree, so the test set up in
Section~\ref{sec:res-structure} resolves squarely in the gateways' favour. Betweenness remains the
rule to use, with Collective Influence a confirming second.

The bridges-over-hubs ordering holds at every rung, but vaccination gets \emph{harder} as the
substrate grows more realistic. Immunising $15$ cities cuts the measles peak by $19\%$ under
betweenness on the sparse air-only network, yet only $12\%$ on the full multilayer, with degree
falling in step ($12\% \rightarrow 8\%$). Two effects compound: the fixed $15$-city budget covers a
smaller share of the larger multilayer, and its land and ferry layers give the outbreak redundant
routes around any removed city. Betweenness keeps its $\approx$$50\%$ relative edge over degree
throughout, so realism does not change \emph{which} cities to protect, only how much budget a given
reduction demands, which is the same redundancy that blunts route closure below.

\subsection{How much budget it takes}\label{sec:res-dose}
Sweeping the winning betweenness budget from $5$ to $200$ cities on the flagship traces a
diminishing-returns curve for every disease (Figure~\ref{fig:dose}): peak reduction climbs from
$\approx$$8\%$ at $5$ cities to $30$--$34\%$ at $200$, passes $\approx$$12$--$13\%$ at the $15$-city
operating point used above, and flattens beyond $\sim$$60$--$120$ cities. Even the best rule, then,
needs a substantial budget to make a large dent, and no small set of cities contains these
outbreaks. The disease ordering is consistent at every budget: the types that confer immunity
(SIR, SEIR, SEIQRD, SEIRS) respond slightly better than the endemic SIS (at $200$ cities, Ebola
$33.8\%$ down to gonorrhea $30.1\%$), because a recovered compartment locks in the vaccination's
gain whereas SIS reinfection steadily erodes it.

\subsection{Closing routes instead of cities}\label{sec:res-interdict}
The second lever closes \emph{routes} rather than immunising cities, and it is where a single-layer
model and the full multilayer part company most sharply (Figure~\ref{fig:interdiction}). Closing
\emph{all} air routes in an air-only model collapses every outbreak to $0.1$--$0.2\%$ of its full
peak (measles $0.18\%$, gonorrhea $0.12\%$): grounding flights ends the epidemic when air is the
only carrier. On the true multilayer substrate the same closure still leaves $32$--$47\%$ of the
peak standing (measles $47\%$, COVID $33\%$, Ebola $32\%$), because land commuting and ferries keep
moving people. A single-layer picture therefore overstates the benefit of a flight ban by more than
two orders of magnitude.

Closing only the top-$10$ hub airports (ranked by betweenness) is weaker still: it leaves
$61$--$71\%$ of the peak in the air-only model and $77$--$90\%$ on the multilayer, and for the slow
endemic type nearly all of it ($90\%$). Removing a handful of bridge airports barely perturbs
an outbreak that also travels by road and sea. Either way the message is the same, that the
realistic multimodal substrate flips the policy conclusion a single-layer model would reach: once
ground and ferry travel are in the picture, no air-route closure contains the epidemic on its own.

\subsection{Deaths averted, and where protection lands}\label{sec:res-deaths}
Two refinements sharpen the vaccination result. First, peak active infection understates what is at
stake for the lethal isolation type, whose case-fatality is high ($\mu\approx0.71$). Reading
cumulative deaths from the model's fatal compartment, bridge-targeting averts $\approx$$7{,}400$
deaths per $100{,}000$ people on the flagship, against $\approx$$4{,}800$ for hub- and
subgraph-targeting and only $\approx$$680$ for random selection (Figure~\ref{fig:deaths-equity}a). The
bridge advantage is therefore \emph{larger} under a mortality metric ($53\%$ more deaths averted
than degree) than under the peak metric ($\sim$$39\%$), because the bottleneck cities betweenness
protects sit on the very routes that would otherwise carry the disease into spared populations.

Second, effectiveness and equity need not trade off on this substrate. Betweenness-targeting places
its cities across the continent rather than onto one nation's hubs: the operating top $15$ span
$12$ countries, and larger budgets ($50$, $200$) extend that cover outward
(Figure~\ref{fig:deaths-equity}b). Degree, the subgraph rule, and both optimal-percolation strategies
instead concentrate their $15$ cities in $10$ countries with $27\%$ in a single one (Germany), a
Gini of $0.26$ against betweenness's $0.15$. The most effective rule is thus also the most
geographically even: targeting structural bridges distributes protection across the continent's
borders rather than piling it onto one nation's hubs.
