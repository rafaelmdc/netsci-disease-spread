\section{Results}\label{sec:results}
We read every outcome in one currency, the peak of active infection, and walk the staged protocol
of Section~\ref{sec:params} one factor at a time: how each disease \emph{spreads} with no
intervention, which node-targeting rule best \emph{defends}, how that winner scales with
\emph{dose}, and what closing routes (\emph{interdiction}) achieves. All runs are on Europe, with
the disease type and the realism ladder (air $\rightarrow$ $+$water $\rightarrow$ $+$land) as the
two cross-cutting axes. Each configuration is a single deterministic run, so absolute counts are
comparative rather than predictive, and we read the differences between scenarios.

\subsection{How each disease spreads}\label{sec:res-spread}
With no intervention every one of the five types takes off on every rung of the ladder
(Figure~\ref{fig:spread}). We report the peak as a fraction of the population, so that substrates
carrying different numbers of people are compared fairly. The peak then orders cleanly by dynamical
class: the immunizing measles type (SIR) is highest, at $\approx$$61\%$ of the population on air
alone, followed by the endemic gonorrhea type (SIS, $\approx$$28\%$) and the latent COVID type
(SEIR, $\approx$$12\%$), with the waning-immunity influenza type (SEIRS) and the
isolation-controlled Ebola type (SEIQRD) lowest at $\approx$$4\%$ each. Latency delays a peak and
isolation caps it, so the two types that add those mechanisms surge least, even though every type
clears its invasion threshold~\cite{colizza:invasion}.

Climbing the realism ladder barely moves that fraction: for every disease it stays within about
three points across air, $+$water, and $+$land (measles $61\%$, $58\%$, $62\%$), which is exactly
why we normalise. In absolute counts the peak roughly doubles from air to the full multilayer
(measles $121$M $\rightarrow$ $213$M; gonorrhea $56$M $\rightarrow$ $97$M), but that rise is almost
entirely a population effect: the land layer pulls inland, non-airport cities into the network,
growing the modelled population from $199$M to $342$M. Per capita, the ladder adds people who can be
infected, not severity. Ferries even lower the peak slightly (measles $61\% \rightarrow 58\%$),
because the coastal cities they add crest out of phase and desynchronise the surge, whereas land's
role is dynamical, synchronising a larger connected population through recurrent
commuting~\cite{balcan:recurrent}. The \emph{temporal} signature, by contrast, belongs to the
disease and not the substrate: the acute measles type peaks within about three weeks (day
$17$--$21$) and burns out, while the endemic gonorrhea type is still climbing toward its plateau at
the four-year horizon.

\subsection{Which vaccination wins}\label{sec:res-defend}
Holding the budget at $15$ immunised cities ($75\%$ coverage) on the multilayer flagship, targeting
the structural \emph{bridges} (betweenness) yields the largest peak reduction for every one of the
five diseases (Figure~\ref{fig:defense}): measles $13.1\%$, the isolation, waning, and latent types
$12.3$--$12.7\%$, and the endemic gonorrhea type $10.8\%$. Targeting the busiest \emph{hubs}
(degree) and the dense-cluster \emph{subgraph} rule (Section~\ref{sec:defend}) tie well below it
($7$--$9.3\%$), while random immunisation is barely distinguishable from doing nothing
($0.5$--$0.8\%$). The ordering bridges~$>$~hubs~$\approx$~subgraph~$\gg$~random is uniform: it holds
for all five disease types and, in the underlying table, at all three rungs, so the win is not an
artefact of one disease or one substrate~\cite{kitsak:spreaders}.

If anything, the realism ladder sharpens this advantage rather than diluting it. The relative gap
between the peak left by hub-targeting and by bridge-targeting grows from $4$--$7.5\%$ on the
air-only network to $\approx$$12\%$ on the full multilayer for every disease (measles $7.5\%
\rightarrow 12.5\%$). Adding land and ferry layers routes yet more traffic through the same
inter-community conduits that betweenness identifies, so removing a bridge airport becomes
\emph{more} valuable, not less.

\subsection{How much budget it takes}\label{sec:res-dose}
Sweeping the winning betweenness budget from $5$ to $200$ cities on the flagship traces a
diminishing-returns curve for every disease (Figure~\ref{fig:dose}): peak reduction climbs from
$\approx$$8\%$ at $5$ cities to $30$--$34\%$ at $200$, passes $\approx$$12$--$13\%$ at the $15$-city
operating point used above, and flattens beyond $\sim$$60$--$120$ cities. Even the best rule, then,
needs a substantial budget to make a large dent, and no small set of cities contains these
outbreaks. The disease ordering is consistent at every budget: the types that confer immunity
(SIR, SEIR, SEIQRD, SEIRS) respond slightly better than the endemic SIS (at $200$ cities, Ebola
$33.8\%$ down to gonorrhea $30.1\%$), because a recovered compartment locks in the vaccination's
gain whereas SIS reinfection steadily erodes it.

\subsection{Closing routes instead of cities}\label{sec:res-interdict}
The second lever closes \emph{routes} rather than immunising cities, and it is where a single-layer
model and the full multilayer part company most sharply (Figure~\ref{fig:interdiction}). Closing
\emph{all} air routes in an air-only model collapses every outbreak to $0.1$--$0.2\%$ of its full
peak (measles $0.18\%$, gonorrhea $0.12\%$): grounding flights ends the epidemic when air is the
only carrier. On the true multilayer substrate the same closure still leaves $34$--$44\%$ of the
peak standing (measles $44\%$, COVID $35\%$, Ebola $34\%$), because land commuting and ferries keep
moving people. A single-layer picture therefore overstates the benefit of a flight ban by two to
three orders of magnitude.

Closing only the top-$10$ hub airports (ranked by betweenness) is weaker still: it leaves
$56$--$70\%$ of the peak in the air-only model and $70$--$99\%$ on the multilayer, and for the slow
endemic type essentially all of it ($99\%$). Removing a handful of bridge airports barely perturbs
an outbreak that also travels by road and sea. Either way the message is the same, that the
realistic multimodal substrate flips the policy conclusion a single-layer model would reach: once
ground and ferry travel are in the picture, no air-route closure contains the epidemic on its own.
