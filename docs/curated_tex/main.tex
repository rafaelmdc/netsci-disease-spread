\begin{abstract}
How an epidemic spreads, and how best to contain it, depends jointly on the disease and on the
transport network it travels, two factors that are usually studied apart. Here we bring them
together on a realistic multimodal substrate, as a controlled and fully reproducible comparison,
delivered as a pipeline with an interactive companion app and scalable to any region. We build a
shared-node multilayer metapopulation of Europe, coupling air as diffusive relocation, land as
recurrent commuting, and water as ferries, and run five disease types parameterised from the
literature, from an immunizing measles-like SIR to a slow endemic gonorrhea-like SIS. Holding all
but the studied factor fixed, we find that targeting bridges (betweenness) consistently beats
targeting hubs (degree) across all five types, and by a wider margin as the network grows more
realistic, while grounding every flight only halves the outbreak, because land and ferry travel
keep carrying it.
\end{abstract}
